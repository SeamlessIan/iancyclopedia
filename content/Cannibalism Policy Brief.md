A five-category risk assessment and proposed legislative framework for community protection, prepared by the Interagency Working Group on Anthropophagic Risk (IWGAR-2026-07).

→ **[View full infographic](/cannibalism-policy-brief.html)**

## Categories covered

1. **Famine / Survival** — consumption of the already-deceased under acute food deprivation
2. **Ritual / Cultural Practice** — consumption sanctioned by cultural or religious norms
3. **Medicinal Consumption** — ingestion of human remains for perceived pharmacological benefit
4. **Sexual / Psychological Disorder** — consumption arising from paraphilia or psychiatric drivers
5. **Warfare / Terror / Coercion** — cannibalism as an instrument of terror or domination in conflict settings

## Key findings

- Peak documented participation rate in a survival event: **48.5%** (Andes Mountains, 1972)
- Largest epidemiological footprint: **>2,700 deaths** from kuru, Fore people, Papua New Guinea (1957–2004)
- Jurisdictions with a unified statutory framework addressing all five categories: **0**

## Dataset

Ten documented cases spanning the 14th century to 2009, across five continents. Evidence tiers range from forensic/physical trial evidence (Dahmer, Meiwes) to 55-year epidemiological surveillance (Fore/kuru) to documentary records (European corpse medicine).
