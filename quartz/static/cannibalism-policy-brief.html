Cannibalism in the Modern Threat Environment
A Five-Category Risk Assessment and Proposed Legislative Framework for Community Protection
Document No.: IWGAR-2026-07 Date: July 2026 Prepared for: Members and Committees of the Legislature Classification: UNCLASSIFIED

Executive Summary

This brief is submitted in response to a working group mandate to assess the nature, prevalence, and distributional pattern of human cannibalism as a public safety concern. The Working Group has reviewed available evidence across documented historical and contemporary cases and presents its findings herein.

Analysis confirms that cannibalism operates through five motivationally distinct channels — famine/survival, ritual/cultural practice, medicinal consumption, sexual or psychological disorder, and warfare or coercion — each with a distinct at-risk population, evidentiary profile, and prevention pathway. The absence of a unified statutory or policy framework addressing all five categories constitutes a gap that this brief seeks to remedy.

Key Findings
  • Five primary threat categories have been identified. Existing law addresses individual criminal acts but contains no provisions specific to the anthropophagic act itself, leaving several categories substantially unaddressed.
  • The highest documented participation rate in a survival-cannibalism event was 48.5% of the at-risk population (Andes Mountains, Argentina, 1972). This figure represents a credible planning benchmark for emergency preparedness frameworks.
  • Ritual and cultural cannibalism, while currently suppressed in all documented populations, produced the largest epidemiological footprint in the evidentiary record: the Fore kuru epidemic (Papua New Guinea, 1957–2004) resulted in over 2,700 deaths within a population of approximately 12,000.
  • Medicinal cannibalism was, for a period spanning approximately six centuries, mainstream therapeutic practice in Western Europe, suggesting that cultural normalisation of the practice is not inherently unprecedented.
  • The most recent documented case in a high-income OECD member state (Germany, 2001) involved an apparently consensual encounter, exposing a statutory gap that the Working Group regards as urgent.
  • No coordinated multi-category prevention framework is currently operative in any surveyed jurisdiction.

Section 3  ·  Risk Assessment

Figure 2 presents a risk matrix plotting each threat category by estimated likelihood of occurrence in a modern, developed-nation context against its potential community impact. The Working Group wishes to draw the attention of Members to the lower-left quadrant, which represents low-likelihood / low-to-moderate-impact threats, and to note that the clustering of categories in this region reflects the partial success of prior (largely informal) suppression measures rather than the inherent tractability of the problem.

Threat likelihood assessments should be understood as context-dependent. Warfare-category cannibalism, assessed as low-likelihood in a stable jurisdiction, registers as high-likelihood in an active conflict zone; the risk matrix reflects conditions in the jurisdiction of this Working Group. Methodological Note — Denominator Selection
Members are advised that participation rates in survival-cannibalism events are highly sensitive to denominator choice. In the Andes case: 16 survivors ÷ 45 initially at risk = 35.6%; 16 ÷ 33 surviving to the decision point = 48.5%; if 16 final survivors all participated, rate approaches 100%. The Working Group uses the at-decision population as the reference denominator, consistent with the principle that individuals who perished before the decision point were not at decision risk. This choice materially affects any headline statistic used in public communications, and Members should be prepared for this methodological point to be raised in debate.

Section 6  ·  Policy Recommendations

The Working Group presents the following recommendations in order of implementation priority. Recommendations are assigned a time horizon and a justifying threat category.

  • 1
    Immediate Priority 1
    Establish a statutory definition of anthropophagy across all five threat categories

    Existing law addresses cannibalism almost exclusively as an aggravating circumstance to homicide, leaving the act itself undefined in most jurisdictions. This creates significant ambiguity in cases involving already-deceased individuals — corpse medicine, funerary endocannibalism, and survival consumption among them. A statutory definition that distinguishes motivated sub-types will allow for differentiated prosecution and provide a principled basis for the recommendations below.

  • 2
    Short-Term Priority 2
    Commission a National Emergency Survival Ethics Protocol with explicit guidance on consumption of deceased fellow passengers

    The 1972 Andes event produced a documented participation rate of 48.5% among survivors and occurred without any pre-existing legal or ethical framework to guide decision-makers. Sixteen individuals survived to rescue in part because of decisions made in the absence of statutory clarity. Emergency preparedness frameworks currently address water, shelter, and signalling; none address this contingency. The Working Group recommends that the relevant aviation and emergency management authorities be tasked with producing guidance within eighteen months.

  • 3
    Short-Term Priority 2
    Address the Meiwes statutory gap: apparent consent does not constitute a complete defence

    The Rotenburg case exposed a gap confirmed by the German courts themselves: the initial conviction under a lesser charge (killing on request) reflected the absence of a specific anthropophagy statute. This jurisdiction should not await a domestic incident before resolving the question of whether consent by the consumed party constitutes a complete defence, a partial defence, or no defence. The Working Group recommends that a Law Commission review be commissioned within the current parliamentary session.

  • 4
    Medium-Term Priority 3
    Integrate anthropophagic ideation screening into existing psychiatric evaluation frameworks

    Both documented psychological-disorder cases in the dataset involved extended concealment periods (thirteen years for Dahmer; planning of indeterminate duration for Meiwes, who placed personal advertisements prior to the incident). Current diagnostic and mandatory-reporting frameworks do not specifically address relevant ideation. The Working Group does not recommend a new clinical category but notes that existing pathways for reporting of risk-relevant ideation should be reviewed for completeness.

  • 5
    Medium-Term Priority 3
    Maintain and increase investment in global food security and famine prevention programs

    Famine/survival cannibalism is, uniquely among the five categories, directly preventable through upstream supply-side intervention. Both documented cases in the dataset occurred under conditions of acute food deprivation that were, in principle, avoidable. The Working Group notes that this recommendation is not exclusively a cannibalism-prevention measure but observes that it would function as one, and recommends that cannibalism prevention be added to the documented co-benefits of existing food security funding arguments.

  • 6
    Ongoing Priority 4
    Strengthen conflict prevention and peacekeeping capacity, with particular attention to sub-Saharan Africa

    Documented warfare-category incidents in Liberia (1993, 1989–97) and the DRC (1998–2003) confirm that active conflict constitutes the primary environmental precondition for coerced and terror-cannibalism. The Working Group observes that this, too, is a recommendation with co-benefits substantially exceeding its cannibalism-prevention function, but reiterates that cannibalism prevention should be included in the documented benefits of conflict prevention investment.

  • 7
    Ongoing Priority 4
    Monitor for emerging cultural or subcultural practices; maintain engagement-based suppression as the primary tool

    The historical record suggests that both Wari' endocannibalism and Fore funerary practice were suppressed through sustained engagement and outside contact rather than enforcement action. The Working Group does not recommend law enforcement as the primary tool in cultural-practice contexts, noting that the epidemiological footprint of the Fore kuru outbreak (>2,700 deaths) illustrates what a failure to engage can cost in public health terms. Continued monitoring by relevant anthropological and public health bodies is recommended, with a reporting duty to government if new practice populations are identified.

Working Group Note
The Working Group is aware that a subset of Members may regard the probability of cannibalism as too low to warrant dedicated legislative attention. The Working Group respectfully notes that the same logic, applied consistently, would have precluded the suppression of European corpse medicine, the engagement with Fore and Wari' populations, and the prosecution of Dahmer and Meiwes — all of which are now regarded as correct. The question before Members is not whether cannibalism is common. It is not. The question is whether the absence of a framework reflects a considered policy choice or an oversight. The Working Group submits that it is the latter.
